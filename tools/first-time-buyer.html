First-Time Buyer Roadmap

Every step from "I think I want to buy" to holding the keys. Florida-specific, no fluff, everything you need to know.

1
2-6 months before

Check Your Credit and Finances

Before you look at a single listing, know your numbers. Pull your free credit report at AnnualCreditReport.com. You need a minimum 620 FICO for conventional loans, but 740+ gets you the best rates. Every 20-point improvement can save you thousands over the life of the loan.

Start saving for your down payment. FHA loans allow as little as 3.5% down, conventional loans typically need 5-20%. But in South Florida, you also need cash for closing costs (2-5% of purchase price), insurance deposits, and moving.

Florida Specific

Florida has several down payment assistance programs: the Florida Hometown Heroes Program offers up to 5% in down payment/closing cost assistance for first-time buyers in over 50 qualifying occupations, with a 0% interest rate and no monthly payments.

What NOT to do: Don't open new credit cards, don't make large purchases, don't change jobs, and don't move money between accounts without a paper trail. Lenders will re-check everything before closing.

2
1-2 months before

Get Pre-Approved (Not Pre-Qualified)

Pre-qualification is a guess. Pre-approval is a commitment. The lender verifies your income, assets, credit, and employment, then gives you a letter stating exactly how much they'll lend you. In South Florida's competitive market, sellers won't take you seriously without one.

Shop at least 3 lenders. Compare not just rates but closing costs, lender fees, and loan estimates. A 0.25% rate difference on a $400K loan is $57/month for 30 years ($20,520 total).

Pro Tip

All credit inquiries for mortgage shopping within a 14-45 day window count as a single inquiry on your credit report. So shop aggressively in a short window. Don't spread it out over months.

Documents you'll need: last 2 years of tax returns, last 2 months of pay stubs, last 2 months of bank statements, W-2s, photo ID. Self-employed? You'll need profit/loss statements and possibly a CPA letter.

3
Active search

Find Your Home

Now the fun part. Work with your agent (that's us) to search listings in your budget and target areas. In South Florida, think hard about: flood zones (affects insurance cost dramatically), HOA rules and financials (especially condos post-Surfside), hurricane impact windows vs. shutters, and commute times (traffic here is real).

Florida Specific

Condo buyers: After the Surfside collapse in 2021, Florida law (SB 4-D) now requires milestone inspections for condos 3+ stories at 25 years (or 30 years if more than 3 miles from coast). Ask for the building's inspection report and reserve study. If reserves are underfunded, expect special assessments.

Look at 10-15 homes minimum before making an offer. Tour at different times of day. Drive the neighborhood at night. Talk to potential neighbors. Check the FEMA flood map for any property you're serious about.

4
The offer

Make an Offer and Negotiate

Your agent will run comps (recent sales of similar homes nearby) to determine a fair offer price. In today's South Florida market (early 2026), buyers have more negotiating power than they've had in years. Don't be afraid to offer below asking if the data supports it.

Your offer includes: price, earnest money deposit (typically 1-3% of purchase price, held in escrow), financing terms, contingencies (inspection, appraisal, financing), requested closing date, and any seller concessions (like covering some closing costs).

Pro Tip

In Florida, the buyer chooses the title company (in most counties). Your agent can recommend one, but shop around. Title insurance and closing fees vary. Also, ask the seller to pay for a home warranty (typically $400-600/year) as part of negotiations.

5
Under contract

Inspection, Appraisal, Insurance

Home inspection ($350-500): Non-negotiable. Hire a licensed inspector. In Florida, pay special attention to: roof condition and age, AC system age (they work hard here), plumbing (especially in older homes with polybutylene pipes), termites and wood-destroying organisms, and mold.

Wind mitigation inspection ($75-150): Florida-specific. Documents your home's wind resistance features. Can save you 15-45% on windstorm insurance. Always get one.

Four-point inspection: Required by most insurers for homes over 20 years old. Covers roof, electrical, plumbing, and HVAC.

Florida Specific

Get insurance quotes BEFORE closing. In South Florida, homeowners + wind + flood insurance can run $6,000-12,000+/year. If you can't get affordable insurance, you may need to walk away. This is the #1 thing that surprises first-time Florida buyers.

6
Closing day

Close on Your Home

Closing in Florida typically happens at a title company (not an attorney's office like some states). You'll sign a LOT of paperwork. Budget 1-2 hours. Bring a valid photo ID and a cashier's check or arrange a wire transfer for your closing costs.

Documentary stamp tax is Florida's transfer tax. On a $400K home, that's $2,800 on the deed alone. Miami-Dade County also adds a surtax of $0.45 per $100 on deeds over $100. Factor this into your closing budget.

7
You own it

Move In and Protect Your Investment

Congratulations, you're a homeowner. Now protect what you've built.

Do This Immediately

File for Homestead Exemption by March 1. This saves you up to $50,000 off your property's assessed value for tax purposes AND caps your assessed value increases at 3% per year (Save Our Homes). File at your county Property Appraiser's office. You must be a permanent Florida resident living in the home as your primary residence. This is free money you lose every year you don't file.

First 30 days: Change all locks, set up utilities in your name, get a home warranty if you don't have one, introduce yourself to neighbors, locate your water shutoff valve and electrical panel, and set up a maintenance schedule (check out our Homeowner Maintenance Calendar).

First year: Start an emergency fund for home repairs (target $5,000-10,000), document your home's contents for insurance, and get familiar with your HOA rules if applicable.
