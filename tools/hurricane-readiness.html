Before the Storm

Preparation is everything. Do these before hurricane season starts (June 1).

0/0
Review your insurance policies Confirm you have wind, flood, AND homeowners coverage. They are separate policies in Florida. Review your deductibles (see calculator below). Essential
Photograph every room and valuable item Document your belongings on video. Open every drawer, every closet. Upload to cloud storage. This is your proof for insurance claims. Essential
Get hurricane shutters or impact windows If you don't have permanent shutters, buy plywood (5/8" minimum) and pre-cut it to your window sizes. Label each piece. Store them accessible. Essential
Build your supply kit Water (1 gallon per person per day, 7 days minimum), non-perishable food, manual can opener, medications, first aid kit, flashlights, batteries, cash in small bills. Essential
Know your evacuation zone Look up your zone at KnowYourZone.com. Know your nearest shelter. Plan two routes out. Fill your car's gas tank early. Essential
Trim trees and clear your yard Dead branches become projectiles. Trim trees away from your home and power lines. Remove or secure anything in your yard that can fly. Recommended
Check your roof Have a professional inspect it. Loose shingles or tiles will fail. Florida law: if your roof is 25%+ damaged, you must bring the entire roof to current code. Recommended
Secure important documents Insurance policies, deed, IDs, passports, birth certificates, medical records. Put originals in a waterproof container. Store digital copies in the cloud. Essential
Get a generator or battery backup Power outages can last weeks. If you get a generator, NEVER run it indoors. Have fuel stored safely. Consider a portable power station as a safer alternative. Recommended
Fill prescriptions early Get at least a 30-day supply of all medications. Keep a list of medications, dosages, and pharmacy contact info in your emergency kit. Essential

During the Storm

What to do when the hurricane is hitting. Safety first, everything else second.

0/0
Stay inside an interior room Bathroom, closet, or hallway with no windows. Stay away from all windows and glass doors. If you hear breaking glass, do not investigate. Essential
Do NOT go outside during the eye The calm is temporary. The back half of the storm often hits harder than the front. Stay inside until authorities give the all-clear. Essential
Keep your phone charged and on low-power mode Conserve battery. Download your county's emergency app beforehand. Text instead of call to keep lines open.
Fill bathtubs with water Use for flushing toilets if water service is lost. Keep your drinking water supply separate and sealed.
Turn refrigerator and freezer to coldest settings Food stays cold longer when power goes out. Keep doors closed. A full freezer holds temperature for about 48 hours.

After the Storm

Recovery steps. Document everything. Move quickly on insurance claims.

0/0
Document ALL damage immediately Photos and videos of everything before you touch it. Timestamp matters. This is your evidence for insurance claims. Essential
File your insurance claim within 48 hours Don't wait. Call your insurer immediately. File wind AND flood claims separately if applicable. Get a claim number in writing. Essential
Watch for downed power lines Assume all downed lines are live. Stay at least 35 feet away. Report them to FPL at 1-800-4-OUTAGE. Essential
Avoid flood water Standing water can be contaminated with sewage, chemicals, and debris. Don't drive through it. 6 inches of moving water can knock you down.
Make temporary repairs to prevent further damage Tarp a damaged roof. Board broken windows. Keep receipts for everything. Your insurance covers reasonable temporary repairs. Recommended
Beware of scam contractors Never pay upfront. Get multiple estimates. Verify licenses at MyFloridaLicense.com. Don't sign an Assignment of Benefits (AOB) without legal advice. Essential
Check on your neighbors Especially elderly neighbors and those who live alone. Community matters most in recovery.

Florida Insurance: What You Actually Need

Most homeowners don't realize they need three separate policies. Standard homeowners insurance in Florida does NOT cover wind or flood damage. Here's the breakdown.

Homeowners (HO-3)

Covers theft, fire, liability, and some water damage. Does NOT cover wind damage from named storms or flooding.

Avg: $4,200/yr in South FL
Does NOT cover hurricane wind damage

Windstorm Insurance

Covers wind damage from named storms. Required by most mortgage lenders. Often through Citizens Property Insurance if private options are too expensive.

Avg: $1,800-3,500/yr
Separate hurricane deductible (2-10% of home value)

Flood Insurance (NFIP)

Covers rising water, storm surge, and overflow. Required if you're in a FEMA flood zone. Available through the National Flood Insurance Program or private insurers.

Avg: $700-2,500/yr
30-day waiting period before coverage starts

Loss of Use / ALE

Covers hotel, food, and living expenses if your home is uninhabitable. Usually included in homeowners policy but check your limits.

Included in HO-3 (check limits)

When a Storm is Coming: Your Timeline

What to do at each stage as a hurricane approaches South Florida.

5 Days Out

Monitor and Prepare

Check your supplies. Review evacuation routes. Start fueling vehicles. Move outdoor furniture inside. Check on elderly neighbors. Top off prescriptions.

3 Days Out

Activate Your Plan

Install shutters or plywood. Fill water containers. Freeze extra water bottles for coolers. Charge all devices and battery packs. Get cash from ATM. Gas up vehicles.

1 Day Out

Final Preparations

Complete all shuttering. Move valuables to interior rooms and higher floors. Pack a go-bag if in an evacuation zone. Set freezer to coldest setting. Do laundry. Charge everything.

Storm Hits

Stay Safe, Stay Inside

Interior room, away from windows. Listen to local officials. Do not go outside during the eye. Text instead of call. Keep a battery radio on local stations.

All Clear

Recovery Begins

Document damage immediately. File insurance claims within 48 hours. Make temporary repairs. Check on neighbors. Avoid floodwater and downed power lines.
